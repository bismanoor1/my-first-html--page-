About the Ocean

The ocean covers more than 70% of our planet's surface and remains one of the least explored environments on Earth.

Stretching across five interconnected basins — the Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, Southern, and Arctic — the ocean regulates climate, produces half of the world's oxygen, and is home to an estimated 700,000 to one million species. Many of them have never been seen by human eyes.

From the sun-drenched coral reefs near the equator to the pitch-black trenches six miles below the surface, the ocean is a world of staggering extremes. Understanding it is not just a scientific endeavour — it is essential to the future of life on Earth.

A Glimpse Below the Surface

Coral reefs occupy less than 1% of the ocean floor, yet they support roughly 25% of all marine species — earning them the nickname "rainforests of the sea."

Ocean Depth Zones

The Five Layers (Unordered List)

  • Sunlight Zone (Epipelagic) — 0 to 200 m; where photosynthesis occurs.
  • Twilight Zone (Mesopelagic) — 200 to 1,000 m; dim light, bioluminescent life.
  • Midnight Zone (Bathypelagic) — 1,000 to 4,000 m; total darkness, extreme pressure.
  • Abyssal Zone — 4,000 to 6,000 m; near-freezing temperatures.
  • Hadal Zone — 6,000 m+; found only in ocean trenches.

Top 3 Deepest Points (Ordered List)

  1. Challenger Deep, Mariana Trench — 10,935 m
  2. Horizon Deep, Tonga Trench — 10,823 m
  3. Sirena Deep, Mariana Trench — 10,732 m
